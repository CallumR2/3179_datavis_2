An Analysis on Global Electricity

By Callum Rafferty | 31503926

This map shows each countries energy consumption as a percentage of the global consumption for different years ranging from 1980 to 2020. Sliding through the years, it is obvious that the United States of America and China have been the worlds largest consumers of electrical energy throughout the time period, while other countries relative consumptions reasonably consistent.

The line graph here breaks up the absolute consumption for different global regions for the same time period of 1980 to 2020. This data shows that the Asia and Ocieana region has been the largest consuming region, with over double the consumption of the next closest region in 2020. Additionally, the Asia and Ocieana region has a steeper positive gradient for net consumption, where other regions have an approximately 0 gradient. It is important to note, these values are absolute and do not account for different population sizes or any other factors.

This bubble chart shows a log-log plot of net Emissions against Electrical Consumption. The plot shows a positive correlation between the net consumption and the Co2 Emissions. Key and interesting countries are highlighted in red.
Being a log-log plot, this indicates there exists a power relationship between the two variables. The sizing of each bubble corresponds to the percentage of energy from renewable sources. We can see that the countries with a higher percentage of renewable energy typically have both consumption and lower emission.

This scatter plot matrix investigates relationships between a nations GDP per capita and other variables. It is imporant to take note of the different scales used here, as different relationships were investigated with log-log or log-linear plots. Key and interesting countries are highlighted in red.
The first chart shows a log-log plot of Consumption per capita against GDP per capita. From this, we can see a positive relationship between the two variables, indicating that the more money a country has per person, the more electricty each person uses on average. This is an intuitively expected relationship, but it is interesting to see that the GDP must increase by a very large amount to see a significant change in the consumption per capita.
The second chart investigates how a countries GDP per capita has affect on the percent of energy they recieve from renewable sources. We can see a negative relationship here, although note this is not a linear relation, as the GDP per Capita axis is on a logarithmic scale. This indicates that wealthier countries do not rely on renewable energy. This also is an intuitive result as we expect developing countries or countries with a smaller population are able to implement renewable energy on smaller scales compared to developed countries heavily dependent on fossil fuels.
The third chart shows how GDP per capita affects access to electricty. We can see that a large majority of countries have 100% access, while less-wealthy countries, specifically those in Africa, have very low access to electricity.
The final chart investigates if a countries GDP per capita has affects on their Co2 emissions. The relation in this chart is not as clear as the others, as it appears that both wealthier and less-wealthy countries have varying emissions. It is interesting to note that the few countries with the largest GDP per capita have relatively low emissions when compared to the next few highest GDP per capita countries.
